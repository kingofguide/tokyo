## Overview

Cyber Kitty is chaos incarnate. She's fragile, unpredictable, and capable of swinging a game completely in a single turn. Her evolutions punish opponents for existing in Tokyo, steal resources directly from their hands, and culminate in one of the most dramatic comeback mechanics in the entire game. She doesn't play the board — she plays with it.

She's rated Advanced because her low resilience means she dies fast in the wrong hands, and several of her best cards require precise timing to use effectively.

## Playstyle

Cyber Kitty is a **Disruptor** and **Smasher**. Unlike Space Penguin who disrupts through denial, Cyber Kitty disrupts through theft and aggression — she takes what she wants directly from opponents while hitting hard enough to keep them off balance.

Her core loop is: **roll ones to amplify → steal resources → hit Tokyo hard → survive long enough to play Nine Lives → win from nowhere**.

She rewards players who enjoy high-variance, high-reward gameplay and aren't afraid of risky turns.

## Evolution Cards

Cyber Kitty's eight evolution cards create a surprisingly cohesive kit around rolling ones, stealing resources, and surviving situations that would eliminate any other monster.

### Meow Missile *(Permanent)*
If you roll at least one 1, add a CLAW to your roll.

A passive damage amplifier that rewards keeping ones in your roll rather than rerolling them away. Most monsters treat ones as wasted dice — Cyber Kitty treats them as triggers. One 1 in your final roll means a free claw on top. Stack with **Mouse Hunter** and **Cat Nip** for turns where rolling ones is actually your primary goal.

### Mouse Hunter *(Permanent)*
If you roll at least one 1, gain 1 STAR.

A free VP every turn you end with a 1 in your roll. Combined with **Meow Missile**, a single 1 now simultaneously gives you a free claw and a free VP. This makes keeping one die showing a 1 a legitimate strategy rather than a concession — and it means Cyber Kitty generates VP passively while doing everything else she's doing.

### Feline Motor *(Permanent)*
During other monsters' movement phases, if Tokyo is empty, you can take control of it instead of the monster whose turn it is.

Tokyo control that costs you nothing on your own turn. If anyone yields or gets eliminated and Tokyo empties during their turn, you can slide in before the active monster gets there — stealing the VP reward and the position entirely. Opponents can't account for it because it triggers on their turn, not yours. One of the most quietly powerful permanents in her kit.

### Play With Your Food *(Permanent)*
When a monster in Tokyo must lose at least 2 HEART from your CLAWS, you can make them lose 2 HEART fewer and instead steal 1 STAR and 1 ENERGY from them directly.

A resource theft evolution that turns every strong claw turn into a choice. Deal full damage, or steal a star and energy from the Tokyo occupant? Against energy-rich monsters like Alienoid or Meka Dragon this is often the better play — 1 STAR and 1 ENERGY stolen is frequently worth more than 2 extra damage. The stolen star directly advances your VP count while setting back theirs.

### Cat Nip *(Temporary)*
Play at the start of your turn. You only have one roll this turn — but double the result.

The highest ceiling single-turn card in Cyber Kitty's kit. Six dice become effectively twelve. Rolling naturally well and doubling it produces turns that no other monster can match — six claws become twelve damage, six lightning bolts become twelve energy. The single-roll restriction means no safety net, so play it on a turn where your first roll is already strong, or when you have **Encased in Ice**-style locks available. Pure high-risk, high-reward.

### Electro-Scratch *(Temporary)*
All other monsters lose 1 HEART.

A board-wide ping that hits everyone simultaneously regardless of Tokyo position. Simple but effective — use it to finish off a weakened monster, pressure multiple opponents at once, or soften up a Tokyo occupant before your claw turn. In a four-player game this deals 3 total damage for free across the table.

### Mega Purr *(Temporary)*
All other monsters give you 1 ENERGY or 1 STAR (they choose).

Mandatory tribute from the entire table. Every opponent must hand over either energy or a VP — their choice, but there's no option to give nothing. In a four-player game that's 3 free resources, and opponents giving energy tend to be protecting stars they can't afford to lose, and vice versa. Best played when multiple opponents are energy-rich and star-rich simultaneously so they have no easy choice.

### Nine Lives *(Temporary)*
If you reach 0 HEART, discard all your cards including evolutions, lose all energy and stars, and leave Tokyo. Gain 9 HEART and 9 STAR, and continue playing.

The most dramatic evolution card in the game. When Cyber Kitty dies, she doesn't — she resets to 9 health and 9 VP instantly. From 9 VP she's two good turns away from winning. The cost is severe — every card, every evolution, all energy gone — but the comeback potential is unlike anything else in the game. Hold this in hand as long as possible and let opponents think they can eliminate you. Trigger it at the right moment and the entire game state flips.

## Tips & Strategies

### Early Game
- Focus on getting **Meow Missile** and **Mouse Hunter** active together — keeping a single 1 in your roll then generates both a free claw and a free VP every turn.
- Roll with a mix of claws and ones rather than going pure claws — Cyber Kitty's triggers reward it.
- Keep **Nine Lives** in hand from the moment you draw it. Never play it early — its value comes from being a surprise.

### Mid Game
- **Feline Motor** changes how opponents think about yielding — once it's active, nobody wants to be the one who empties Tokyo for you.
- **Play With Your Food** decisions should favour stealing over damage against energy-heavy monsters whose engines you want to disrupt.
- Start eyeing low-health opponents as **Nine Lives** triggers — let them think they're close to eliminating you.

### Late Game
- **Nine Lives** at the right moment — ideally when you're at 1-2 health and opponents are about to pile on — launches you to 9 VP instantly and resets the game on your terms.
- **Cat Nip** is your best closer if your health is stable — doubling a strong natural roll can end the game in a single turn.
- **Mega Purr** late forces opponents to sacrifice resources they need for their own closing turns.

### Best Evolution Combos
- **Meow Missile + Mouse Hunter + Cat Nip** — double your dice result with both one-triggers active. A single 1 in that doubled roll gives you a free claw and a free VP on top of everything else you rolled.
- **Play With Your Food + Feline Motor** — steal resources from Tokyo occupants with your claws, then slide into Tokyo on their turn the moment they yield. You benefit from their exit twice.
- **Nine Lives + Electro-Scratch or Mega Purr** — after Nine Lives triggers and you're at 9 VP, immediately play a resource grab or board ping to accelerate the closing push.

### Card Priorities (Base Market)
1. **Even Bigger** — extra health extends how long you can hold Nine Lives in reserve before needing it
2. **Regeneration** — same reasoning, keeps you alive longer at critical health thresholds
3. **Apartment Building** — post-Nine Lives at 9 VP this can close the game in one or two turns
4. **Nova Breath** — hits all outside-Tokyo monsters, pairs with your disruption identity

## Common Mistakes

- Rerolling ones away out of habit — Meow Missile and Mouse Hunter both need at least one 1 to trigger
- Playing **Nine Lives** defensively when your health is merely low — save it for the moment you actually hit 0, not as a precaution
- Using **Cat Nip** on a mediocre first roll — if your initial six dice aren't already strong, doubling a bad roll is still a bad roll
- Ignoring **Feline Motor** once it's active — keep track of Tokyo's status during other players' turns, it triggers when you least expect it
- Using **Mega Purr** when opponents are resource-poor — it needs a table full of energy and stars to be worth a temporary evolution slot
- Forgetting that **Play With Your Food** is a choice each time — defaulting to full damage against a star-rich opponent is almost always wrong
